## Part 1: The Question and Why It Matters

### What you're studying

When researchers want to know which cell types in a tissue are genetically relevant to a disease, the standard approach is to combine two data sources: a genome-wide association study (GWAS), which tells you which genetic variants are associated with the disease, and a single-cell RNA-seq atlas of the relevant tissue, which tells you which genes each cell type expresses. A class of computational methods (scDRS, scPagwas, seismic, FUMA, S-MAGMA, sc-linker, and others) integrates these two data sources to nominate "disease-critical" cell types.

The closest existing precedent is Watanabe et al. 2019 (*Nature Communications*), which collated 43 publicly available scRNA-seq datasets and used MAGMA gene-property regression with a within-/between-dataset conditional procedure to identify trait-cell-type associations across 26 traits. For inflammatory bowel disease, their analysis nominated four independent cell types: microglia, blood cell, endothelial cell, and marrow B-cell. None of these are colon-specific. Their corpus is heavily brain-biased (29 of 43 datasets) and contains no human disease-tissue-matched colon scRNA-seq, because in 2019 those atlases didn't exist at scale. Their framework is therefore structurally blind to the colon-resident cell types — BEST4+ enterocytes, inflammatory fibroblasts, colonic Tregs, goblet cells — that the modern UC scRNA-seq literature has spent five years arguing are central to disease pathology.

A more recent benchmark by Chu et al. (2025, medRxiv preprint) compared modern per-cell methods across 33 traits and 10 atlases, but each trait-atlas pair is run once, and the comparison contrasts single-organ vs. whole-body atlases at different granularities — not multiple disease-tissue-matched atlases of the same tissue. Lakkis et al. 2024 (*Frontiers in Immunology*) ran scDRS, scPagwas, and scGWAS on UC, but used a single atlas. Oliver et al. 2024 (*Nature*) and a December 2025 IBD integration preprint merge multiple atlases into a single mega-atlas and prioritize once, which is a different question.

What has not been done: directly quantifying rank concordance of modern per-cell GWAS scoring methods (scDRS, scPagwas, seismic) across independent disease-tissue-matched atlases. We provide such a comparison for ulcerative colitis. The motivating question for a practitioner is concrete: when an investigator runs GWAS-driven cell-type prioritization on whatever colon atlas they happen to have, how predictive is that result of what they'd find on a different colon atlas? If concordance is high, the field's single-atlas results are reliable. If concordance is low, single-atlas studies should be interpreted as exploratory and multi-atlas validation should become standard practice.

### Why ulcerative colitis specifically

Three reasons. First, the IBD/UC GWAS is large and stable — de Lange et al. 2017 has full summary statistics publicly available, and there's nothing about the underlying genetics being in flux. Second, the colon mucosa has at least three independent, high-quality, public scRNA-seq atlases that were generated by different labs using somewhat different protocols, which is exactly what you need for a cross-atlas comparison. Third, UC has already been used as a test case in the 2024 inflammatory benchmark (Lakkis et al., Frontiers in Immunology), so you can position your work as the natural cross-atlas extension of that paper.

### What the deliverable looks like

A bioRxiv preprint by end of semester, with a clear narrative: "We ran three modern per-cell prioritization methods on three independent ulcerative colitis colon atlases against the same UC GWAS, with a parallel MAGMA gene-property track for methodological grounding. We measured cross-atlas agreement using rank correlation, top-k Jaccard overlap, and FDR-significance concordance. We found X% of cell-type-disease associations replicate across atlases. The associations that replicate are characterized by Y. We further compare to a broad multi-tissue atlas to test whether tissue-matched concordance differs from broad-atlas concordance, and recommend Z for future studies."

The realistic publication target is *Bioinformatics Advances*, *NAR Genomics and Bioinformatics*, *BMC Bioinformatics*, or *Frontiers in Genetics*. None require Nature-tier novelty; all accept solo undergraduate first authorships when the work is clean. Median review time is 2–4 months.

### 2.1 What a GWAS is, and what summary statistics actually look like

A genome-wide association study tests, for each of roughly 8–12 million common genetic variants (SNPs) across the genome, whether carrying one allele vs. the other is statistically associated with the trait being studied. For each SNP, the test produces an estimated effect size (β if continuous trait, log-odds-ratio if binary), a standard error, and a p-value. With 8 million SNPs tested, the conventional genome-wide significance threshold is p < 5×10⁻⁸ (a Bonferroni-style correction for the effective number of independent tests in the genome). Modern complex-trait GWAS routinely identify hundreds of "hits" — independent loci passing this threshold.

Two facts about GWAS that drive everything downstream:

**Most signal is sub-significant but real.** Diseases like UC are highly polygenic — thousands of variants each contribute a tiny incremental effect. The variants that pass p < 5×10⁻⁸ are the tip of an iceberg; many genuinely associated variants sit between p = 10⁻⁴ and 10⁻⁸. Cell-type prioritization methods exploit this: they look at the *aggregate* signal across many genes, not just genome-wide-significant ones. This is why the methods need full summary statistics, not just the lead SNPs from each locus.

**SNPs are correlated.** A SNP doesn't sit alone — it inherits as part of a haplotype with its physical neighbors. Two SNPs that are 5 kb apart and on the same haplotype block will show nearly identical association p-values, even if only one of them is causally driving the signal. This correlation pattern is called **linkage disequilibrium (LD)**, and it's the central statistical headache of post-GWAS analysis: you cannot treat nearby SNPs as independent observations.

**The summary statistics file.** A typical GWAS summary stats file is a tab-separated text file with one row per tested SNP. The columns vary across consortia, but you should expect to see (or be able to compute):

```
SNP         CHR  BP        A1  A2  FRQ_A1   BETA      SE       P          N
rs1234567   1    100123    A   G   0.234    -0.0421   0.0089   2.31e-06   62000
rs9876543   1    100456    T   C   0.891    0.0102    0.0234   0.6644     61500
...
```

`A1` is the effect allele (β is reported relative to it), `A2` is the other allele, `FRQ_A1` is its allele frequency, `BETA` and `SE` are the effect estimate and its standard error, `P` is the association p-value, `N` is the per-SNP sample size. For binary traits like UC, BETA is the natural log of the odds ratio. Some files give Z-scores instead of BETA/SE; Z = BETA/SE.

**For your project — de Lange 2017 UC.** Two reliable sources for the file:

1. **GWAS Catalog** (recommended primary, mirrored, stable URL): https://www.ebi.ac.uk/gwas — search "GCST004133" for IBD, "GCST004131" for UC, "GCST004132" for Crohn's. Download the harmonized summary statistics (`.tsv.gz`, ~150 MB).
2. **IIBDGC consortium portal** (https://www.ibdgenetics.org), which also hosts the original files in their as-published format.

Sample sizes you'll see in the de Lange paper: ~25,042 IBD cases, ~34,915 controls (combined IBD); UC-specific subset is ~12,366 cases, ~33,609 controls. Use **UC-only** as your primary GWAS — that's the trait you're prioritizing — and IBD-combined as a sensitivity analysis.

**Quality-checking the file before you do anything else.** Three things to verify on download:

- *Genome build.* The summary stats are in **GRCh37 (hg19)** coordinates. Every reference dataset you'll use later — MAGMA's gene-location file, the 1000 Genomes LD reference, your scRNA-seq atlases (which use gene symbols, not coordinates, so this matters less for them) — must match. If anything is in GRCh38, you must lift over to one common build before proceeding. UCSC's `liftOver` tool does this; allow a half-day for it.
- *Allele consistency.* Some downstream tools (scDRS doesn't care about this; PRS pipelines do) need A1/A2 to be ordered consistently. Check that the file has effect-allele columns clearly labeled.
- *Imputation quality, INFO score.* GWAS files often include a per-SNP imputation quality metric. Drop SNPs with INFO < 0.6 if the column exists. Also drop SNPs with MAF < 0.01 (rare variants are unstable in cell-type prioritization).

You don't need to understand the upstream genotyping or imputation. You need a clean, GRCh37, EUR-ancestry summary stats file with full p-values for ~8M+ common variants.

### 2.2 From SNPs to gene scores: MAGMA in detail

Cell-type prioritization methods operate on **genes**, not SNPs. So the first transformation in every pipeline is SNP → gene. The standard tool is **MAGMA** (de Leeuw 2015, *PLOS Comp Bio*), a precompiled C++ binary you download from the CTGLab website. It runs in three steps.

**Step 1 — SNP-to-gene annotation.** MAGMA assigns each SNP to a gene if the SNP falls within a window around that gene's transcription start/end sites. The default is 0 kb (i.e., only SNPs inside the gene body), but most cell-type prioritization papers use a window — typically **10 kb upstream + 10 kb downstream**, sometimes larger. The choice matters: a wider window captures more regulatory variants (most GWAS hits are non-coding regulatory) but also dilutes the gene-level signal with unrelated SNPs.

```bash
magma --annotate window=10,10 \
      --snp-loc snps.loc \
      --gene-loc NCBI37.3.gene.loc \
      --out step1_annot
```

`snps.loc` is a 3-column file from your GWAS (rsid, chromosome, BP), and `NCBI37.3.gene.loc` ships with MAGMA — it lists every protein-coding gene with its TSS/TES coordinates.

**Step 2 — Gene-based test.** For each gene, MAGMA aggregates the p-values of all SNPs assigned to that gene into a single gene-level p-value, accounting for LD between those SNPs. The default test (`snp-wise=mean`) computes the mean χ² statistic across the gene's SNPs, then evaluates that statistic against the null distribution given the LD structure. The LD reference is critical: MAGMA needs a panel of individual-level genotypes from the same ancestry as the GWAS to estimate which SNPs are correlated. For European-ancestry GWAS like de Lange 2017, use the **1000 Genomes Phase 3 EUR** reference panel (also downloadable from the CTGLab site, ~1 GB).

```bash
magma --bfile g1000_eur \
      --pval gwas.pval N=45975 \
      --gene-annot step1_annot.genes.annot \
      --out step2_gene
```

This produces `step2_gene.genes.out`, with one row per gene and columns including `GENE` (Entrez ID), `NSNPS` (number of SNPs in the gene), `ZSTAT` (standardized association strength), and `P` (gene-level p-value).

**Step 3 — Convert to scDRS gene set.** The scDRS pipeline expects a `.gs` file: a tab-separated file with trait names and ranked gene weights. Use scDRS's helper script `scdrs munge-gs`, or manually take the top 1,000 genes by ZSTAT and weight them by their ZSTAT. The convention from the scDRS paper is **top 1,000 MAGMA genes, weighted by Z-score**.

**Sanity check at this point.** Open `step2_gene.genes.out` and look at the top 20 genes by Z-score. For UC, you should see things like *IL23R*, *JAK2*, *TYK2*, *NKX2-3*, *IL12B*, *SMAD3*, *STAT3*, and many genes from the MHC region on chromosome 6. *NOD2* appears more in Crohn's-specific results than UC — that's expected. If your top 20 looks nothing like the published UC GWAS lead-gene table, your pipeline has a bug (likely a build mismatch or column-misread issue).

You'll run MAGMA once, generate one `.gs` file, and reuse it across all four atlases.

### 2.3 scRNA-seq atlas data: the AnnData object and what's inside

A scRNA-seq dataset is a sparse cell × gene count matrix paired with metadata. In Python, it lives in an **AnnData** object (`.h5ad` file format), which is what scanpy and scDRS both expect.

**Anatomy of an AnnData object.** Conceptually:

```
AnnData object
│
├── X                    cell × gene count matrix (sparse, scipy CSR)
│                        e.g., 250000 cells × 22000 genes
│
├── obs                  pandas DataFrame, one row per cell
│   ├── cell_type        e.g., "CD4 T cell", "Goblet cell"
│   ├── donor_id         e.g., "UC_001"
│   ├── disease_status   e.g., "healthy" / "UC"
│   ├── sample_id        e.g., "UC_001_R3"
│   ├── n_genes          per-cell QC: how many genes detected
│   ├── n_counts         per-cell QC: total UMI count
│   └── pct_mito         per-cell QC: % of counts from mitochondrial genes
│
├── var                  pandas DataFrame, one row per gene
│   ├── gene_symbol      e.g., "IL23R"
│   ├── ensembl_id       e.g., "ENSG00000162594"
│   └── highly_variable  whether the gene is in the HVG set
│
├── obsm                 multi-dimensional embeddings (UMAP, PCA)
└── layers               alternative count matrices (raw, normalized, etc.)
```

**Sparsity.** Each cell expresses only a few thousand genes detectably; the matrix is ~95% zeros. This is why it's stored as CSR (compressed sparse row) — a 250k-cell × 22k-gene matrix is ~5 billion entries dense but only ~250 million non-zero entries (a 20× memory saving).

**Counts vs. normalized expression.** The raw count matrix is what you want in `adata.X` for scDRS — it expects raw or log-normalized counts and applies its own normalization internally. Some atlases ship with already-normalized values (log1p-CPM-scaled) in `X` and the raw counts in `adata.layers["counts"]`; in that case, you swap them: `adata.X = adata.layers["counts"]`. Check before running.

**What the QC metrics mean and why they matter.**

- *n_genes per cell.* Cells with very few detected genes (<200) are often empty droplets or dying cells. Cells with very many (>6000–8000) may be doublets. Most published atlases have already filtered these.
- *pct_mito.* High mitochondrial content (>20%) indicates cell stress or death; these are usually filtered out.
- *Doublet detection.* Tools like Scrublet flag cells that are likely two cells captured in one droplet. Should already be done in published atlases.

You shouldn't need to re-run QC — the atlas authors did it — but you should *verify* by checking these distributions when you load the atlas. If something looks off (e.g., a chunk of cells with 50% mito content), document it and decide whether to filter further.

**Cell-type labels.** Each atlas's `obs` table has a column with the cell-type assignment per cell. The column name varies (`cell_type`, `celltype`, `subset`, `cluster_label`, `Cluster`, `final_annot`...). The labels themselves are inconsistently formatted across atlases — see section 2.6 on harmonization.

**Gene symbols vs. Ensembl IDs.** Atlases use either gene symbols (the human-readable names, like "IL23R") or Ensembl IDs (stable identifiers like "ENSG00000162594"). Symbols are easier to read but can change over time and have synonyms; Ensembl IDs are stable but ugly. scDRS expects gene symbols matching its `.gs` file. If your atlas has Ensembl IDs, convert with the `mygene` Python package or scanpy's gene-mapping helpers. Make all symbols uppercase to avoid case-sensitivity bugs.

**Loading and inspecting an atlas in Python:**

```python
import scanpy as sc

adata = sc.read_h5ad("data/atlases/smillie_2019.h5ad")
print(adata)                     # shape, what's in obs/var/layers
print(adata.obs.columns.tolist())
print(adata.obs["cell_type"].value_counts().head(20))
print(adata.var.columns.tolist())
print(adata.X[:5, :5].toarray()) # check it's actually counts (integers)
```

If `adata.X` shows floats with decimals like 0.6931, you're looking at log-normalized values, not raw counts. Look in `adata.layers` for the raw matrix.

### 2.4 The cell-type prioritization frameworks, in real detail

You'll use three modern methods plus a Watanabe-style sanity track. Each takes the same two inputs (a GWAS gene set and a scRNA-seq atlas) but builds rankings using different statistical machinery. Understanding *how* they differ is essential for interpreting cross-method disagreement when you see it.

#### 2.4.1 scDRS (Zhang et al. 2022, *Nature Genetics*)

**One-line summary:** for each cell, compute a polygenic enrichment score for the disease, then test cell types for elevated mean scores.

**Mechanics.** For each cell *c* and the trait gene set *G* (the top 1,000 MAGMA genes, weighted by Z-score), scDRS computes a raw score:

```
raw_score(c) = Σ_g [w_g × normalized_expression(c, g)]
```

where *w_g* is the gene's MAGMA Z weight and the expression is normalized within the cell. Then it generates **1,000 control gene sets** by sampling random genes matched on (a) mean expression and (b) variance of expression across all cells. The cell's normalized score is:

```
score(c) = (raw_score(c) - mean of control scores) / (sd of control scores)
```

This gives a per-cell Z-score that's robust to "this gene set just contains highly-expressed genes." The matched-null is the key statistical innovation — it's why scDRS results aren't dominated by housekeeping bias.

**Cell-type-level inference (the Monte Carlo group test).** To get cell-type-level p-values from per-cell scores, scDRS uses a permutation-style group analysis: the observed mean score within a cell type is compared to the distribution of mean scores over the 1,000 control gene sets, evaluated within the same cell type. This produces a per-cell-type p-value and an FDR-corrected q-value.

**What you actually run:**

```bash
# Score every cell
scdrs compute-score \
    --h5ad-file data/atlases/smillie.h5ad \
    --h5ad-species human \
    --gs-file data/gwas/uc_top1000.gs \
    --gs-species human \
    --cov-file data/atlases/smillie_covariates.tsv \
    --flag-filter-data True \
    --flag-raw-count True \
    --out-folder results/scdrs/smillie/

# Cell-type group analysis
scdrs perform-downstream \
    --h5ad-file data/atlases/smillie.h5ad \
    --score-file results/scdrs/smillie/UC.score.gz \
    --group-analysis cell_type \
    --out-folder results/scdrs/smillie/
```

The `--cov-file` is a TSV of per-cell technical covariates to regress out (n_genes, sequencing depth, percent mito) — this matters because scDRS needs the per-cell scores to be free of confounding by sequencing depth.

**Output.** Two files. The `.score.gz` has one row per cell with normalized scores and per-cell p-values. The `.scdrs_group.cell_type` has one row per cell type with a group p-value, an FDR q-value, and a heterogeneity statistic.

**Compute and memory.** scDRS docs report ~3 hours and ~60 GB for a 1M-cell dataset. For 250k-cell atlases, expect ~30–45 min and 15–20 GB. The bottleneck is the 1,000-control-gene-set Monte Carlo, not the per-cell scoring.

**Important: scDRS does not require disease tissue.** The question is "which cell types preferentially express the genes implicated by GWAS," which is answerable on healthy tissue. Most published applications run scDRS on healthy reference atlases (Tabula Sapiens, Human Cell Atlas) rather than disease-state atlases. For your project, both atlas types are useful — disease-state atlases (Smillie, Mennillo) capture disease-relevant cell states; healthy atlases (Pan-GI subset) capture baseline cell-type structure.

#### 2.4.2 scPagwas (Ma et al. 2023, *Cell Genomics*)

**One-line summary:** model GWAS Z-scores as a linear function of cell-type-specific pathway-activity scores.

**Mechanics, in more depth than scDRS because the framework is non-obvious.** scPagwas first computes, for each cell, an activity score for each of ~200 KEGG pathways — essentially the average expression of pathway-member genes in that cell. This converts the cell × gene matrix into a much smaller cell × pathway matrix. Then, for each cell, it builds a polygenic regression: the gene's MAGMA Z-score is regressed against the gene's "membership-weighted pathway activity" in that cell. The slope of this regression is the per-cell **pathway-aware GWAS score (PAGWAS score)**. Cells where the slope is large and positive are cells where high-pathway-activity genes happen to be the high-GWAS-Z-score genes — i.e., cells where the disease-associated genes are concentrated.

**Cell-type-level inference.** Aggregate PAGWAS scores within each cell type, compute a TRS (Trait-Relevant Score), and assess significance via bootstrap.

**What this does differently from scDRS.** scDRS scores cells based on gene-set membership (binary or weighted membership in the top-1000 list). scPagwas scores cells based on whether *the pathway structure* of GWAS signal aligns with that cell's pathway activity. The two methods can disagree when GWAS signal is concentrated in genes that share pathway membership (favors scPagwas) vs. when GWAS signal is concentrated in highly-cell-type-specific genes regardless of pathway (favors scDRS).

**Practical reality.** scPagwas is the highest-friction tool you'll touch. See the detailed warning in section 4 (Week 7) — installation is fragile, runtime is multi-day per atlas, memory exceeds 64 GB on full-size data, output format requires glue code, and documentation lags the code. If you can get clean results on two atlases, that's enough; don't burn writing time on getting all four to work.

#### 2.4.3 seismicGWAS (Lai et al. 2025, *Nature Communications*)

**One-line summary:** compute a robust cell-type "specificity score," then regress GWAS Z-scores against it.

**Mechanics.** The seismicGWAS specificity score for gene *g* in cell type *c* is, roughly, the average expression of *g* in cells of type *c*, normalized by the gene's mean expression across all cells, with a variance-stabilizing transform that makes the score robust to re-clustering. The published demonstration: if you re-label or re-cluster a homogeneous cell population in different ways, the specificity scores barely move. (This robustness is *within* one atlas, not across atlases — that's exactly the point your project addresses, and it's why seismicGWAS slots in cleanly.)

Then for each cell type, the method runs a regression of MAGMA gene Z-scores on the specificity scores: `Z_g ~ β_0 + β × specificity(g, c) + ε`, with β > 0 indicating that high-specificity genes for cell type *c* tend to have high GWAS Z-scores.

**What this does differently from scDRS and scPagwas.** scDRS uses gene-set membership; scPagwas uses pathway structure; seismicGWAS uses cell-type specificity. The seismicGWAS regression is mathematically closer to MAGMA gene-property analysis (section 2.4.4) than to scDRS — which is part of why including seismicGWAS gives you a useful methodological bridge between modern per-cell methods and classical Watanabe-style methods.

**Practical reality.** Clean R package (`devtools::install_github("ylaboratory/seismicGWAS")`), well-documented, fast (minutes to ~1 hour per atlas), memory-friendly. If scPagwas dies, seismicGWAS is your backup second method, and the comparison "scDRS vs seismicGWAS" is still a real cross-method comparison because the underlying statistical machinery is genuinely different (per-cell scoring with matched null vs. cell-type-level specificity-score regression).

#### 2.4.4 MAGMA gene-property regression (Watanabe-style sanity track)

**One-line summary:** the regression Watanabe 2019 used. Cell-type-mean expression as a predictor of MAGMA gene Z-scores.

**Mechanics.** For each cell type *c*, compute the mean expression of every gene in cells of type *c*: `E_c[g]`. Then for that cell type, run the regression:

```
Z_g = β_0 + β_E × E_c[g] + β_A × A[g] + Σ_k β_k × confounder_k + ε
```

where *Z_g* is the MAGMA gene Z-score, *A[g]* is the average expression across all cell types (controlling for "gene is just expressed everywhere"), and the confounders are gene length and other technical features. The coefficient *β_E* tests whether cell type *c*'s expression specifically predicts disease association — a one-sided test for *β_E* > 0 is the cell-type enrichment p-value.

Watanabe's three-step framework adds: (1) within-dataset conditional analysis to handle correlated cell types, and (2) between-dataset conditional analysis to test which cell types are independently associated when you account for what's already explained by other datasets. For your project, you only need step 1 plus the basic regression — the cross-atlas conditional analysis is the modern equivalent of what your concordance metrics measure.

**What you run.** Either via the FUMA web interface (free, http://fuma.ctglab.nl, easy upload of `.h5ad` and GWAS, but you can't script multiple runs cleanly) or via the MAGMA command-line binary using the `--gene-covar` flag with cell-type-mean-expression vectors. The CLI version is what you want for a reproducible pipeline.

**Why it's the sanity track and not the positive control.** The four modern methods test "is GWAS signal concentrated in this cell type's signature genes?" with various definitions of *signature*. MAGMA gene-property tests a related but mechanically different thing: "is GWAS signal concentrated in genes that are highly expressed in this cell type, controlling for general expression?" Disagreement between MAGMA gene-property and scDRS/seismicGWAS doesn't mean either is broken — they ask subtly different questions. But if your MAGMA gene-property analysis on Smillie can't recover *any* sensible signal (i.e., your B-cell category and endothelial-cell category both come back as p ~ 0.5), your gene-mapping or LD reference is probably broken, and you need to fix it before week 7. That's the diagnostic value: it distinguishes "everything's working but the methods disagree" from "my pipeline has a bug."

### 2.5 The reproducibility question, formally

Suppose you run scDRS on Smillie with the de Lange UC GWAS. You get a ranked list of 15 cell types: at the top, "Inflammatory monocytes" with q < 0.001; further down, "Transit-amplifying epithelial cells" with q = 0.04; at the bottom, "Smooth muscle cells" with q = 0.83. Now you run the same pipeline on Kong 2023. Do you get the same ranking?

The answer is a number, several numbers actually, from a few different angles. Each angle exposes a different aspect of agreement.

**Spearman rank correlation.** Compute the rank of each cell type within each atlas (1 = most associated, K = least). The Spearman ρ between the two rank vectors is +1 if rankings are identical, 0 if uncorrelated, -1 if exactly reversed. Use Spearman not Pearson because raw p-values span many orders of magnitude and Pearson over-weights the tail. Implementation: `scipy.stats.spearmanr(ranks_atlas1, ranks_atlas2)`.

**Top-k Jaccard.** Of the top *k* cell types each atlas nominates, how many are in both lists? `J = |A ∩ B| / |A ∪ B|`. Use *k* = 5 and *k* = 10. Jaccard is conservative — a cell type ranked 6th in one atlas and 7th in another both miss the top-5 even though the rankings barely differ — but it matches how the field actually reads results ("the paper said Tregs are #1").

**Cohen's kappa for FDR-significance.** Convert each cell type to a binary "passes FDR < 0.05?" call in each atlas. Build a 2×2 contingency table:

```
                Atlas 2
                Sig    Not-sig
Atlas 1 Sig      a       b
       Not-sig   c       d
```

Cohen's κ measures agreement beyond chance:

```
p_o = (a + d) / (a + b + c + d)         # observed agreement
p_e = ((a+b)(a+c) + (c+d)(b+d)) / N²    # chance agreement
κ = (p_o − p_e) / (1 − p_e)
```

κ = 0 means agreement at chance; κ = 1 means perfect agreement. The conventional thresholds: κ < 0.4 poor, 0.4–0.6 moderate, 0.6–0.8 substantial, > 0.8 almost perfect. Compute κ via `sklearn.metrics.cohen_kappa_score`.

**One important edge case.** If almost every cell type passes FDR < 0.05 in both atlases (e.g., for highly polygenic, immune-dominant traits like UC where every immune cell type is "associated"), κ will be artificially low because there's almost no "Not-sig" count to anchor against. Report the marginals (`a+b`, `a+c`) along with κ so readers can interpret it correctly. Consider using a more stringent threshold (FDR < 0.01) as a sensitivity analysis if marginals are unbalanced.

**The headline summary table** for the paper looks like this:

| Comparison | Spearman ρ | Jaccard top-5 | FDR concordance (κ) | Marginals |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smillie vs Kong (scDRS) | ? | ? | ? | n_sig in each |
| Smillie vs Mennillo (scDRS) | ? | ? | ? | ... |
| Kong vs Mennillo (scDRS) | ? | ? | ? | ... |
| (same rows for seismicGWAS, scPagwas if survived) | | | | |

Plus a parallel cross-method-within-atlas table (scDRS vs seismicGWAS on Smillie, etc.) so you can attribute disagreement to "atlas effect" vs. "method effect."

### 2.6 Cell-type label harmonization, the actually-hard part

You cannot compare rankings across atlases until you can match cell types across atlases. This is where projects of this shape spend most of their unanticipated time. Walk through it carefully.

**The problem, concretely.** Atlas A (Smillie) has 51 cell-type labels including "Inflammatory Monocytes," "MT-hi" (mitochondrial-high cells), "Cycling TA" (cycling transit-amplifying), and "Goblet." Atlas B (Kong) has different labels: "Mono" (broader category), no MT-hi (filtered out), "TA" (no cycling/non-cycling distinction), "Goblet." Atlas C (Mennillo) has yet other labels with 36 fine cell populations focusing on mononuclear phagocytes. Just direct string-matching gives you maybe 5–8 cell types; you need a principled way to match the rest.

**Strategy 1: Cell Ontology (CL) via scOntoMatch.** The Cell Ontology is a curated tree of biological cell types maintained by OBO Foundry. Every cell type has a CL: identifier (e.g., CL:0000815 for "regulatory T cell"). The R package **scOntoMatch** maps each atlas's labels to CL: terms and aligns them at a common granularity in the tree. If atlas A says "memory CD4 T cell" (CL:0000897) and atlas B says "CD4 T cell" (CL:0000624, the parent), scOntoMatch climbs the tree to the common ancestor and creates a unified label.

```r
library(scOntoMatch)
ont <- get_OBO("https://obofoundry.github.io/ontology/cl.owl")
mapping <- ontoMatch(
    onto = ont,
    metas_tab = list(smillie = smillie_obs, kong = kong_obs, mennillo = mennillo_obs),
    anno_col = "cell_type"
)
```

scOntoMatch will work for ~70% of cells out of the box. The remaining 30% need manual mapping — atlases use idiosyncratic labels that don't directly hit the ontology ("Cycling TA" doesn't match a CL: term cleanly; "fibroblast WNT2B+ Fos-hi" definitely doesn't).

**Strategy 2: Manual mapping with a CSV crosswalk.** For everything scOntoMatch misses, build a hand-curated mapping table:

```
atlas     original_label            broad_label              fine_label
smillie   Inflammatory Monocytes    Monocyte/Macrophage      Inflammatory monocyte
smillie   M2 Macrophages            Monocyte/Macrophage      M2-like macrophage
smillie   Cycling TA                Stem/TA                  Cycling TA
kong      Mono_CXCL10               Monocyte/Macrophage      Inflammatory monocyte
kong      Mono_FCN1                 Monocyte/Macrophage      Classical monocyte
kong      TA_cycling                Stem/TA                  Cycling TA
mennillo  MNP_Inflammatory          Monocyte/Macrophage      Inflammatory monocyte
...
```

You build this in a spreadsheet, with one tab per atlas's original labels, and merge against a "canonical_labels.csv" master. Plan for ~3–4 days of work. The mapping is biology, not engineering — you have to read the atlas papers to understand what each label means, which markers define it, and which canonical label it best maps to.

**Strategy 3: Marker gene-based confirmation.** Once you have a candidate mapping, validate it by checking that the marker genes for "Inflammatory monocyte" in atlas A really are also enriched in the cells you've labeled "Inflammatory monocyte" in atlas B. Use scanpy's `sc.tl.rank_genes_groups` and check overlap of top markers. If atlas A's "Inflammatory monocyte" top markers (CXCL10, IL1B, CCL3) are not enriched in your atlas-B match, your mapping is wrong.

**Two granularity levels.** Maintain mappings at two levels:

- **Broad** (~10–15 categories): T cell, B cell, plasma cell, monocyte/macrophage, dendritic cell, mast cell, neutrophil, NK cell, ILC, enterocyte, goblet cell, stem/TA, fibroblast, endothelium, smooth muscle. Every atlas has every category. This is your headline analysis.
- **Fine** (~30–50 categories): subdivided by activation state, lineage subset, etc. Some atlases have subsets others don't (Smillie has 4 fibroblast subtypes; Kong has 2). Handle missing categories by reporting concordance pairwise on the intersection.

**A red flag to watch for.** If your broad-level mapping forces you to merge cell types that the atlas authors clearly intended as biologically distinct (e.g., "stromal" lumping fibroblasts + endothelium + smooth muscle), you've gone too coarse. Better to drop those cells entirely from a comparison than to compare apples to oranges.

**The hard deadline.** End of week 5. If you don't have at least three atlases harmonized at the broad level by the end of week 5, descope to two atlases and ship.

### 2.7 The statistical comparison metrics, with formulas and edge cases

This section gives you the implementation-level detail for what's described conceptually in 2.5. Open Python and have these snippets ready.

**Spearman correlation:**

```python
from scipy.stats import spearmanr

# rankings: dict of cell_type -> rank, one per atlas
common = sorted(set(ranks_a.keys()) & set(ranks_b.keys()))
rho, pval = spearmanr([ranks_a[c] for c in common], [ranks_b[c] for c in common])
```

Edge case: if some cell types are present in only one atlas, they're dropped from the Spearman comparison. Report the count of common cell types alongside ρ.

**Top-k Jaccard:**

```python
def top_k_jaccard(scores_a, scores_b, k=5):
    top_a = set(sorted(scores_a, key=scores_a.get)[:k])  # smallest p-value = top
    top_b = set(sorted(scores_b, key=scores_b.get)[:k])
    return len(top_a & top_b) / len(top_a | top_b)
```

Edge case: ties at rank *k* (two cell types with identical p-values). Break ties by Z-statistic; if still tied, use the more conservative interpretation (smaller intersection).

**Cohen's kappa:**

```python
from sklearn.metrics import cohen_kappa_score

def fdr_concordance(qvals_a, qvals_b, threshold=0.05):
    common = sorted(set(qvals_a.keys()) & set(qvals_b.keys()))
    sig_a = [qvals_a[c] < threshold for c in common]
    sig_b = [qvals_b[c] < threshold for c in common]
    return cohen_kappa_score(sig_a, sig_b), sum(sig_a), sum(sig_b)
```

Always report the marginals (`sum(sig_a)`, `sum(sig_b)`) alongside κ. If they're highly imbalanced (e.g., 14/15 vs 12/15), κ is unstable and should be supplemented with a sensitivity analysis at FDR < 0.01.

**Cross-method within atlas:** the same Spearman computation, but comparing scDRS rankings to seismicGWAS rankings within (say) Smillie. This isolates "method disagreement" from "atlas disagreement" — a critical decomposition for the discussion.

**Bootstrap confidence intervals.** For each concordance metric, compute a bootstrap CI by resampling cells (with replacement) within each atlas, re-running the cell-type p-value computation, and re-computing the metric. 100 bootstrap iterations give you a usable 95% CI. This is computationally expensive (each iteration re-runs scDRS), so do it on the headline numbers only, not every cell-attached comparison.

**The headline summary table:**

| Comparison | Spearman ρ (95% CI) | Jaccard top-5 | FDR concordance (κ) | Marginals (n_sig) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Smillie vs Kong (scDRS) | 0.71 [0.62, 0.79] | 3/5 | 0.58 | 9 / 8 |
| Smillie vs Mennillo (scDRS) | 0.66 [0.55, 0.76] | 3/5 | 0.51 | 9 / 7 |
| Kong vs Mennillo (scDRS) | 0.78 [0.69, 0.85] | 4/5 | 0.65 | 8 / 7 |

### 2.9 What success looks like

The honest target by week 12: a working pipeline, a clean set of cross-atlas concordance results, a draft preprint, and a public GitHub repo. The headline finding will be one of three patterns:

1. **Findings replicate well** (Spearman ρ > 0.7, top-5 Jaccard > 0.6, κ > 0.6 across atlas pairs at the broad-cell-type level). Reassuring for the field. Paper says: "GWAS-driven cell-type prioritization is robust to atlas choice for major UC-relevant cell types; single-atlas studies are likely to give reliable results at this granularity." This is also the *most* boring outcome — but a confirmed-null is still publishable, especially with the gene-window robustness check, the MAGMA gene-property sanity track, and the tissue-matched-vs-broad-atlas comparison giving the paper distinguishing technical contributions.

2. **Findings replicate well at coarse resolution but poorly at fine resolution** (broad ρ > 0.7 but cell-state ρ < 0.5). The most likely outcome based on the French 2025 mouse-brain finding and basic statistical reasoning (rare cell types have noisier estimates). Paper says: "Broad cell-type findings are reliable across UC atlases. Fine-grained cell-state findings are not, and should be interpreted cautiously without multi-atlas validation." This is the most informative outcome for the field.

3. **Findings don't replicate even at coarse resolution.** Most surprising and most impactful. Paper says: "Single-atlas cell-type prioritization studies in UC should be interpreted as exploratory; multi-atlas validation should be considered standard practice." Would force a re-evaluation of how Lakkis 2024, the Chu benchmark, and other single-atlas studies interpret their own findings.

All three outcomes are publishable. There is no scenario where this project produces a result that's not worth writing up.

The thing that *would* be a failure: spending so long on harmonization or scPagwas debugging that you don't write the paper. Watch the week-5 and week-7 hard deadlines. A 2-atlas, scDRS-only paper with a MAGMA gene-property sanity track and a tissue-matched-vs-broad-atlas comparison is publishable, even if it's not your full original ambition.

You need three independent disease-tissue-matched UC colon atlases for the core comparison, plus one broad multi-tissue gut atlas for the sensitivity panel. Below are the recommendations and the fuller candidate pool, with notes on what to actually pick.

#### Recommended core: three tissue-matched UC colon atlases

**Atlas 1 — Smillie et al. 2019** (the canonical UC reference; **strongly recommended primary**)
- Citation: Smillie CS et al. "Intra- and inter-cellular rewiring of the human colon during ulcerative colitis." Cell 178:714–730.

**Atlas 2 — Kong et al. 2023** (**recommended**)
- Citation: Kong L et al. "The landscape of immune dysregulation in Crohn's disease revealed through single-cell transcriptomic profiling in the ileum and colon." Immunity 56:444–458.
- Note: this is technically a Crohn's atlas but includes UC samples and colon tissue. You'll need to filter to UC + colon samples (6 healthy + 6 active UC donors after filtering, per the GSE214695 metadata).
- Access: GEO accession GSE214695

**Atlas 3 — pick one; default Mennillo 2024, fallback Devlin/Zhao 2023.** This slot is the one to think hardest about because Devlin (originally listed) is on the small side.

- ***Mennillo et al. 2024*** (***recommended primary for slot 3***) — *Nature Communications* 15:1493, "Single-cell and spatial multi-omics highlight effects of anti-integrin therapy across cellular compartments in ulcerative colitis." Larger than Devlin, recent (2024), distinct cohort from Smillie/Kong, and the anti-integrin treatment angle adds biological signal in immune compartments that's relevant to UC. Available via GEO.

#### Recommended broad-atlas comparator (Atlas 4, sensitivity panel)

A core sensitivity panel of the project compares your colon-specific cross-atlas concordance against concordance computed using a broad multi-tissue atlas. This directly tests whether tissue-matched atlases give different concordance signatures than broad multi-tissue atlases — a question Watanabe and Chu cannot answer from their data, and the most distinctive contribution of the paper.

- ***Pan-GI Cell Atlas / Oliver et al. 2024*** (***recommended primary***) — *Nature* 635:699–707. The healthy reference contains ~1.1M cells from 189 healthy donors across the GI tract, uniformly processed via scAutoQC, integrated with scANVI, annotated via CellTypist. Available at gutcellatlas.org and CELLxGENE Discover as `.h5ad`. Filter to large-intestine cells for the comparison. This is the most powerful broad-comparator currently available.

  **Important caveat — partial overlap with your core atlases.** The Pan-GI healthy reference integrated 25 datasets, including Smillie 2019. If you compare your Smillie-based scDRS run against your Pan-GI-based scDRS run, the two are not statistically independent. There are two clean options: (a) restrict the Pan-GI broad-atlas slot to the donors that are *not* from Smillie/Kong/Devlin/Mennillo (check the `donor_id` and `study` metadata when loading), or (b) acknowledge the overlap as a methods limitation and report concordance both ways. Verify donor lists in week 4 when you're examining metadata.

- *HCA Gut Cell Atlas / Elmentaite et al. 2021* (*Nature* 597:250–255) — predecessor to Pan-GI. ~140k healthy intestinal cells across regions and developmental stages. Use only as fallback if you can't get Pan-GI to load cleanly.
- *Tabula Sapiens (TS 1.0 / TS 2.0)* — body-wide multi-tissue atlas. Not recommended: TS 1.0's large-intestine slice is small (a few thousand cells from one or two donors), TS 2.0 is larger but the release status and annotations are uneven, and you'd be using a body-wide atlas to compare against gut-specific atlases when you have a gut-specific broad atlas (Pan-GI) available. Skip unless both Pan-GI and HCA Gut fail.

#### Sidenote: how many atlases is enough?

You want a minimum of 3 disease-tissue-matched atlases plus the broad-comparator (4 total). If you can confidently process more (5 or 6), pairwise concordance estimates get tighter, and the robustness regression in week 9 has more (cell type, atlas pair) data points to fit on. But each additional atlas adds a full harmonization burden, and the cell-type harmonization step is the single most likely thing to blow up your timeline. Three tissue-matched + one broad is the sweet spot. If you get to end of week 5 and have only two cleanly harmonized, that's the minimum viable paper — Lakkis 2024 had one fine-grained UC atlas, so two with concordance metrics is already a strict improvement on the literature.

**Adding Atlas 4 means four sets of cell-type labels to harmonize, not three. The week-5 hard deadline still applies.** If harmonization is running over budget by end of week 5, treat Atlas 4 as a stretch panel to be added in week 10 rather than as core, and ship the 3-atlas tissue-matched comparison as the primary result.

### Week 7: Run scPagwas and seismicGWAS
**Goal: results from two more methods, for cross-method robustness**

Both are R packages, both take the GWAS summary stats (or MAGMA output) and the scRNA-seq data, both output cell-type-level statistics.

**The scPagwas problem, in detail.** scPagwas is the highest-risk component of the project and you should plan around the assumption that it may not survive contact with your data. Several concrete failure modes:

- *Compute time scales badly.* The Lakkis 2024 benchmark reported roughly 45 hours per atlas on UC-scale data (~228k cells) for scPagwas, versus a few hours for scDRS on the same data. With four atlases (Smillie, Kong, Devlin, Pan-GI subset) that's potentially 7–10 days of wall-clock time even running in parallel on a cluster — and that's only the successful runs. Failed runs eat the same time before they error out.
- *Memory blow-ups.* scPagwas builds pathway-level activity matrices (cells × pathways) and then runs a polygenic regression over them. On atlases above ~200k cells, peak memory routinely exceeds 64GB, which is above what Colab Pro+ provides and what many shared HPC nodes default to. You need to either subsample aggressively (which weakens the comparison to the full-resolution scDRS/seismicGWAS runs) or request high-memory queue access in advance.
- *Installation is fragile.* The GitHub-only install (`devtools::install_github("dengchunyu/scPagwas")`) pulls a long chain of Bioconductor and CRAN dependencies, several of which have version conflicts with R 4.3+ (the version most package managers default to). Pinning works but takes a half-day of trial and error. Conda/mamba's `r-scpagwas` doesn't exist; you're building from source.
- *Documentation is uneven.* The function signatures changed between scPagwas v1.x and v2.x in ways that aren't always reflected in the README. The Lakkis benchmark used v1.2 and the Ma 2023 paper code used a still-different version. Expect to read the function source code to figure out what arguments mean.
- *Output format is non-standard.* scDRS and seismicGWAS both produce per-cell-type p-values in similar tabular format. scPagwas outputs a "TRS" (trait-relevant score) with bootstrap-derived significance that needs separate post-processing to be comparable. You'll write glue code.

The combined effect: scPagwas can plausibly cost you an entire week of debugging plus another week of compute, with no guarantee of success on all four atlases. If by **end of week 7 (Friday)** you don't have scPagwas results on at least two atlases, descope it. Run scDRS + seismicGWAS as your two primary methods (these alone give you cross-method robustness — per-cell scoring vs. specificity-regression — and they use the same MAGMA z-score input, which makes the comparison clean). The MAGMA gene-property sanity track from week 6 still serves as your methodological grounding. A two-method comparison across four atlases with the broad-comparator panel is publishable. Don't let scPagwas eat the writing weeks.

If you run into trouble with seismicGWAS (less likely — it's a clean, well-documented R package with the Yao lab actively responsive on GitHub issues), the same logic applies: ship what you have.

**Deliverable**: cell-type rankings from scDRS, seismicGWAS, and (if it survived) scPagwas for all atlases.

### Week 7.5: GWAS gene-window robustness check
**Goal: pre-empt the obvious reviewer comment**

Before moving to concordance analysis, run MAGMA gene-based analysis a second time with an extended gene window (50 kb upstream, 50 kb downstream) instead of the default 10 kb on each side. This produces a second `.genes.out` file and, downstream, a second top-1000-gene set for scDRS and a second z-score vector for seismicGWAS.

Then on one atlas (Smillie is the natural choice) re-run scDRS and seismicGWAS with the 50 kb gene set and check whether your cell-type rankings move materially. Report the cross-window Spearman ρ as a one-paragraph supplementary section: "Cell-type rankings are robust to MAGMA window choice (Spearman ρ = X.XX between 10 kb and 50 kb runs on Smillie)."

Window choice is a known sensitivity in MAGMA-based pipelines and a likely reviewer comment. Showing your concordance results don't depend on it costs you maybe half a day and pre-empts the issue. If the rankings *do* move materially (Spearman < ~0.85), that's itself a finding worth a sentence in the discussion — and it means you should think harder about which window to feature in the headline results.

**Deliverable**: a `.genes.out` file at 50 kb window, a one-paragraph supplementary table comparing 10 kb vs 50 kb cell-type rankings on Smillie.

### Week 8: Concordance analysis
**Goal: the headline numbers**

Compute and tabulate, for each pair of atlases and each method:
- Spearman correlation between cell-type rankings
- Top-5 and top-10 Jaccard overlap
- Cohen's kappa for FDR-significance
- Within-atlas cross-method concordance (scDRS vs scPagwas vs seismic on the same atlas)

Make the headline figure: a 3×3 heatmap (atlas × atlas) of Spearman correlations, faceted by method. This should be roughly the most-shared figure in the eventual paper.

Make the second figure: a forest plot of which specific cell types are robustly nominated (significant in 3/3 atlases by ≥2/3 methods) vs. atlas-specific (significant in only 1 atlas).

**Deliverable**: 2–3 main-text-quality figures with headline concordance numbers.

### Week 9: Tissue-matched vs. broad-atlas comparison + what predicts robustness
**Goal: turn the descriptive concordance numbers into something interpretable, and run the distinguishing sensitivity panel**

Two analyses this week.

**Analysis 1 — broad-atlas comparison.** Run scDRS (and seismic if it's still in scope) on the broad-atlas comparator (HCA Gut Cell Atlas or Tabula Sapiens, whichever survived week 4 verification), restricting to large-intestine cells where applicable. Compare two concordance numbers:

- *Tissue-matched concordance*: pairwise Spearman across Smillie, Kong, Devlin
- *Tissue-matched-vs-broad concordance*: each tissue-matched atlas vs. the broad atlas

Are they the same? If tissue-matched concordance is materially higher than tissue-matched-vs-broad concordance, this is direct evidence that disease-tissue-matched analyses give different answers than broad multi-tissue analyses, and that the field should use disease-tissue-matched atlases. If they're similar, broad multi-tissue atlases are an acceptable substitute, which is a different (and also publishable) finding.

This is the panel that makes the paper distinctive — it answers a question Watanabe and Chu cannot answer from their data because they don't have multiple tissue-matched atlases of one tissue.

**Analysis 2 — what predicts robustness.** For each (cell type, atlas pair) combination from week 8, investigate what features predict whether the finding replicates. Candidate predictors:

- **Cell-type abundance in the atlas** — do rare cell types have noisier estimates and lower replication?
- **Marker gene specificity** — do cell types with more uniquely expressed marker genes replicate better?
- **Cross-atlas marker gene agreement** — does the cell type's gene signature look similar across atlases (use MetaNeighbor)?
- **Atlas size** (number of cells, number of donors) — does more data help?
- **Granularity** — broad vs fine cell-type labels

Fit a simple regression: outcome is "this cell type's significance was concordant across atlas pair," predictors are the above features. The point isn't a perfect predictive model, it's an interpretable analysis: "we find that X and Y predict robustness; the biggest single factor is granularity; cell types abundant in fewer than 2% of cells are essentially unreliable."

**Deliverable**: tissue-matched vs broad-atlas comparison results (1–2 figures), a regression analysis of robustness predictors (1 figure).

### Week 10: Optional fine-grained cell-state analysis (stretch goal)
**Goal: extend from broad cell types to fine-grained cell states**

If weeks 1–9 went smoothly, push into the fine-grained level. Re-do the concordance analysis at the cell-state level (e.g., "Th17 cells" rather than "T cells").

Expect this to be where things break down. Cell states are atlas-specific in their definitions, harder to align across atlases, and (per French 2025) genuinely don't replicate as well as broad cell types. This is fine — it's a finding, not a failure.

If you descoped Atlas 4 (broad multi-tissue) at the week-5 hard deadline, week 10 is the time to bring it back as a stretch panel. If neither is feasible, skip and proceed straight to writing.

**Deliverable**: optional cell-state-level analysis or stretch-recovery of Atlas 4. If skipped, document this as a known limitation in the discussion.

### Weeks 11–12: Writing and polish
**Goal: a complete preprint draft**

Standard manuscript structure: abstract, introduction, results, discussion, methods, figures, supplementary. ~3,000–5,000 words.

Section sketch:
- **Abstract** (250 words). One sentence on the question, one sentence on the approach, two sentences on the findings, one sentence on the implications.
- **Introduction** (500–800 words). Frame the cell-type prioritization landscape; cite Watanabe 2019's 43-dataset MAGMA framework and note that its IBD result is structurally blind to colon cell types; note Chu 2025 (single-atlas-per-trait) and Lakkis 2024 (single-atlas UC); state the specific gap your work fills (rank concordance of modern per-cell methods across disease-tissue-matched atlases). Avoid "rigorously," "for the first time," "nobody has ever" — replace with specific citations and specific gaps.
- **Results** (1,500–2,000 words). Four subsections: MAGMA gene-property sanity check on Smillie, cross-atlas concordance for broad cell types, tissue-matched vs broad-atlas comparison, what predicts replication, and (if done) cell-state-level analysis.
- **Discussion** (500–800 words). Interpret findings, note limitations (3–4 atlases is still small; UC-only generalization is unclear; harmonization is imperfect; methods test slightly different statistical hypotheses), suggest future work.
- **Methods** (1,000–1,500 words). Detailed enough that someone could reproduce your work from the manuscript alone.

Make a polished GitHub repo with:
- Code, organized by step
- Notebooks that reproduce each main figure
- A clear README with installation, data download, and run instructions
- A final environment file (pyproject.toml + renv.lock)

Submit to bioRxiv. The submission is free, takes a few hours, and gets you a citable preprint by end of semester. Then submit to *Bioinformatics Advances* or *NAR Genomics and Bioinformatics* in the following weeks.

**Deliverable**: bioRxiv preprint, public GitHub repo.
